The problem, in plain terms
Your immune system runs on sensors. When a cell spots a virus, a bacterium, or a foreign molecule, it's usually one of a family of proteins called pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) that trips the alarm and starts the immune response. Researchers want to activate or block these sensors deliberately — to make vaccines work better, to fight cancer, or to treat autoimmune disease.

The problem is that testing whether a molecule hits a specific receptor requires synthesizing it in a chemistry lab first — days to weeks of work, hundreds to thousands of dollars per compound, most of which fail. ImmunoBind gives you a binding-strength estimate in seconds before you spend a cent on synthesis. It won't replace the lab assay, but it cuts dead-end candidates out early so the compounds that do get made are far more likely to work.
Receptor groups
Toll-like receptors (TLR1–10)
The primary bacterial and viral sensors on immune cells. TLR1/2 and TLR2/6 recognize lipopeptides from bacteria; TLR4 recognizes LPS (the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria); TLR3/7/8/9 detect viral nucleic acids. Heavily targeted for vaccine adjuvants and sepsis drugs.
STING & cGAS
STING (Stimulator of Interferon Genes) and its upstream sensor cGAS detect foreign or misplaced DNA inside a cell — a sign of viral infection or a cancer mutation. STING agonists are one of the most active areas in cancer immunotherapy right now, with several compounds in clinical trials.
RIG-I & MDA5
These sensors sit inside the cell and detect double-stranded RNA — the kind that appears when a virus is replicating. Activating RIG-I or MDA5 triggers a strong antiviral interferon response. Drug candidates targeting these receptors are being explored for influenza, COVID-19, and RSV.
